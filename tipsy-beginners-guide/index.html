What actually is Tipsy?

Tipsy is a chatbot platform where you write the character and the AI does the talking. You set up who the character is, how they speak, and what world they live in — then anyone can chat with them.

💡

The one thing to understand before anything else Tipsy has no memory between sessions. There's no database. Every chat starts fresh. Your prompt is literally everything the AI knows — if you don't write it down, the AI won't know it.

This sounds scary but it's actually freeing: you're in complete control. A well-written prompt means a consistent, in-character bot. A vague prompt means a vague bot. That's the whole deal.

How Tipsy works

The four fields — and what they're actually for

When you create a bot on Tipsy, you fill in four separate boxes. Each one does a completely different job. The AI only reads one of them — and it's not the one most beginners think.

Name The title of your bot Users + AI
Storefront card copy and the character's name in one. Whatever you put here becomes {{char}} — the name the AI uses in chat. Bot names on Tipsy typically combine the character name with a descriptor. If the title is longer than the character's actual name, you can override what the AI calls them in the Background field.
Vera, The Mechanic Next Door Lucius Kai Blackwood-Voss Damian | Tsundere Dark Romance
Description The hook that sells the bot Users only
Tipsy labels this "Publicly seen." It appears on the bot's card and before the first message loads. It does two jobs: sell the premise to people browsing, and tell the user who they are in the story. If your bot has a specific setup for the user's character — their relationship to the bot, what they know, where they're starting from — this is where it goes.
You moved into the apartment without reading the lease properly. Turns out the previous tenant never left. The voice started small — lights flickering, your name in an empty room. Now you're not sure if you want it to stop.
Opening The first message in the chat Users + AI
The literal first thing that appears when someone opens your bot. Both the user and the AI read this. Don't start at the beginning of the story — drop the user into a scene that's already happening.
*The kitchen light is on when you get home. You definitely didn't leave it on.* "You're late," a voice says from somewhere behind the fridge. "I counted."
🖼️
You can add images to the Opening

Host your image somewhere publicly accessible (Imgur, Discord CDN, your own server), then drop it into the Opening field using markdown image syntax:

![](https://www.website.com/imagename.jpg)

Use good quality images with no watermarks (your own creator mark is fine). Tipsy displays images at 9:16 ratio — anything else will crop. Size your image to fit before you upload.

Background Everything the AI actually uses AI only
Tipsy calls this the Background field. This is where your whole prompt goes — the world, the character, the rules. The AI reads nothing else. It's hidden from users by default (the "Public" toggle keeps it private). If something isn't in here, the AI doesn't know it.
Setting, character personality, how they speak, what they want, story rules — all of it goes here.
⚠️

The most common beginner mistake Writing your character description in the Description field and wondering why the bot doesn't act right. The Description is for users. The Background field is for the AI. They are completely separate.

The Background field

Writing your prompt — in the right order

Your Background field is read top to bottom by the AI. Structure matters. Here's the order that works, and what goes in each part.

1

Formatting rules

Tell the AI how to write. The standard on Tipsy: dialogue in double quotes, actions in asterisks, inner thoughts in single quotes. Paste this block at the very top of every Background field you ever make — it's universal.

2

Roleplay rules

The AI plays the character. The user plays themselves. The AI should never write the user's actions or feelings — that's the user's job. Also include: don't break character, don't summarise the plot as a list, one exchange at a time.

3

Your character

The core of everything. Cover: appearance, personality, how they speak, what they want on the surface, what they actually want underneath, and how they relate to the user. Give example dialogue lines — "how they speak" in the AI's hands means sample sentences, not adjectives.

4

Setting

Where and when is this story? What does the world look like? What's the vibe, the atmosphere, the rules of the place? One solid paragraph gives the AI the context it needs to keep the scene consistent.

Writing craft

The rules that actually make a difference

These aren't style preferences. These are the things that separate a bot that stays in character across 50 messages from one that starts drifting after five.

✦ Stop before the user acts

Every AI response should end before the user needs to say or do something. Don't write the user's reaction. Write up to the moment they need to respond — then stop.

✦ Show, don't tell personality

Don't write "he is sarcastic." Write a sarcastic example line. "Of course. Brilliant plan." does more work than ten adjectives describing the same trait.

✦ Give them something they want

Characters with a clear goal — even a hidden one — stay consistent. A character who wants nothing is impossible to write reliably across a long chat.

✦ Drop the user mid-scene

The opening shouldn't start at "the beginning." It should start inside something already happening. The user should feel like they arrived late to a scene, not watched one start from zero.

✦ Use formatting consistently

Pick a formatting style and stick to it throughout the whole prompt. Inconsistent formatting confuses the model and the output will waver to match.

✦ Say it once, clearly

If you wrote something once, don't repeat it in another section. Repetition creates contradictions. Say it once, say it clearly, move on.

Worked example

A complete minimal prompt

Here's what all four fields look like for a simple original character. No trackers, no time skips — just a working bot, minimum viable.

Name field — becomes {{char}} in chat
Vera, The Mechanic Next Door
Description field — shown to users, not the AI
She fixes things. Cars, mostly. People, occasionally, if they're lucky enough to catch her on a good day. She doesn't do small talk, but she'll hand you a coffee without being asked. You live next door to Vera's workshop and you've seen her every day since you moved in. At first it was just a nod or a wave, then a quick chat, now you find yourself spending free moments with her whenever you can.
🏷️

Tags are separate Below the Description you'll find Tipsy's tag system — a set of clickable chips (Slow Burn, OC, Modern, etc.). Pick up to 10 that match your bot. These help people find it when browsing. They're not part of the prompt and the AI doesn't read them.

Opening field — users AND the AI read this
*The garage bay is open when you walk past at 7am. She's already under the hood of something, half her body disappearing into an old pickup truck. Tool-stained overalls, radio going, no acknowledgement that you exist.* *Then without looking up:* "Coffee's on the bench if you want it. Mug on the left is clean."
Background field — the AI only, hidden from users by default
FORMATTING RULES {{char}} is Vera. Use double quotes for dialogue "like this" Use asterisks for actions *like this* Use single quotes for thoughts 'like this' Stop before the user needs to act. Never describe the user's actions or feelings. --- ROLEPLAY RULES The AI plays Vera only. Never play {{user}} or decide their reactions. Never break character. Never summarise past events as a list. One exchange at a time. --- VERA — THE MECHANIC Appearance: Late 20s. Dark hair, usually tied back badly. Oil on her hands most of the time. Practical, not fashionable. Personality: Dry, self-contained, observant. Not unfriendly — just economical with words. Notices more than she lets on. Voice: Short sentences. Deadpan. Never explains herself. Asks questions she already knows the answer to. Surface goal: Be left alone to work. Hidden motivation: She is paying attention. She always was. Before you publish

Launch checklist

Run through this before hitting publish. All of these checked means your bot is ready.

Name is short, specific, and searchable
Description sells the premise in 2–3 sentences (no character instructions in it)
Opening drops the user mid-scene — not at the very beginning of events
Opening ends before the user needs to act or respond
Background field has: formatting rules, roleplay rules, character, setting — in that order
Character entry includes actual example dialogue lines
The prompt never tells the AI to play {{user}}
Tags are selected (up to 10) from Tipsy's tag system
You've read the opening message and it sounds like your character
🚀

What to do after you publish Test it yourself first. Have a 10-message conversation. Notice what drifts, what feels off, what the bot says you didn't intend. That's your edit list. Almost every bot needs one round of fixes after first contact — that's normal, not failure.
